1. Why a custom norming study?

To manipulate relational influence in the memory experiments, the authors needed homonyms paired with two unambiguous semantic associates — one biasing the dominant meaning, one biasing the non-dominant meaning. The non-dominant pair (e.g., MUSCLE–CALF) is hypothesized to carry higher relational influence because the associate must actively pull attention away from the default meaning.

Off-the-shelf norms exist (Twilley et al., 1994; Eligio & Kaschak, 2020; Becker, 1980), but the authors argue that a free-association paradigm has a self-undermining problem: the response itself can be ambiguous.

People may generate SAVE after seeing BANK, but they may not generate BANK after seeing SAVE — instead they may generate HERO. So SAVE alone wouldn't reliably activate the BANK meaning at study time.

The custom design measures four specific properties under a single consistent procedure, allowing the authors to filter out cases like SAVE.

2. The four screening questions

Words flow through four sequential analyses. Failing one removes the word (and often its homonym group) from later analyses.

1
How well-known is the word?

If most people don't know what COW means, COW can't activate anything in CALF. Words people don't recognize are dropped.

2
Do the associates relate to their homonym?

Verifies that COW reliably evokes a meaning of CALF. Quantified as Yule's Q on a 2×2 of "related to a meaning" judgments vs. an unrelated control word.

3
Are the associates unambiguous themselves?

The crucial check. SAVE relates to BANK, but SAVE itself has multiple senses; if it activates the wrong one (HERO), it can't cue BANK reliably. Measured by reversing the pair — define SAVE first, then judge whether BANK relates to your definition.

4
Does the homonym have a dominant meaning?

A 50/50 homonym wouldn't allow a clean dominant-vs-non-dominant contrast in the memory experiments. Yule's Q on the 2×2 of meaning-A vs. meaning-B associate judgments quantifies the imbalance.

3. Try the task yourself

The actual study had 91 trials per participant. Below is a faithful re-implementation of five of them, drawn from the homonym sets the thesis actually uses. The procedure follows section 2.1.3 of the thesis: see a target word, wait 1.5 seconds, type a definition (or "pass"), then judge how a comparator word relates to that definition with one of four options.

Use the keyboard if you'd like — Enter to advance and submit, 1–4 to pick an option. (You can also click.)

4. Set the thresholds yourself

Each of the four indices was given a screening threshold by inspecting its histogram for a "steep change" — the kind of decision that's easier to second-guess than to defend. The histograms below use simulated data designed to match the qualitative shape of Figure 2.3 in the thesis. Drag the sliders to see how the surviving stimulus pool changes.

The pipeline cascades: failing the unknowability screen drops a homonym and all its associates. If two associates of the same meaning are screened out, the whole homonym group is dropped (since each meaning needs at least one associate).

The thesis's defaults retained 52 homonyms and 104 associates. Try moving the relatedness or unambiguity threshold by 0.05 in either direction — the surviving pool can swing by 10–20 stimuli, which would cascade through the entire memory experiment design.

5. Critique · Participants didn't overlap

The norming sample isn't the experimental sample.

If the value of a custom norming study is that the norms are calibrated to the people you'll later test, this thesis doesn't get that benefit. The four studies drew from different populations entirely:

StudyN (retained)Source
Norming91Prolific · English L1 · ≥70% approval
Exp 1 — Item Recognition140U of Alberta intro psych · course credit
Exp 2 — Associative Recognition100Prolific · same screening as norming
Exp 3 — One-list AR96Prolific · same screening as norming

Notably, Experiment 1 used a different recruitment pool entirely. Even where Exp 2/3 share Prolific with the norming study, no individual overlap is enforced.

So what is the custom norming study actually for? The defensible answer is the unambiguity index — the SAVE-versus-HERO check that off-the-shelf free-association norms don't provide. Word frequency and dominance, the other indices, could plausibly have been pulled from existing databases (SUBTLEX, Twilley et al.) without much loss. Re-measuring those is the kind of redundancy that a participant-overlap argument would otherwise justify.

6. Critique · Thresholds set by eye

"Steep change" is not an algorithm.

Each threshold was placed by visual inspection of its histogram, looking for a knee. No algorithm (e.g., Kneedle, derivative-based detection), no cross-validation, no sensitivity analysis. The thesis is forthright about this for the unambiguity index in particular:

"This threshold may have been too liberal since it may have allowed words that were close to being too ambiguous to pass. However, we chose this threshold to maximize the size of the stimulus pool."

A second oddity: the framing flips between indices. For unknowability, "above the elbow = outliers we exclude." For the other three, "below the elbow = outliers we exclude." Both are defensible but the rationale isn't symmetric.

The interactive in section 4 directly demonstrates the problem: shifting any threshold by a small amount swings the surviving pool by 10–20 stimuli. Stimulus selection has cascading consequences for any effect size estimated downstream — and in a thesis whose central findings are null, that sensitivity matters more than usual. The thesis design doesn't include a sensitivity analysis showing the predictions are robust to threshold choice.
