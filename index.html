1. Why static items are not enough

A homonym such as CALF can mean "baby cow" or "leg muscle." Which interpretation pops to mind depends on what you saw it next to: COW–CALF evokes the animal; MUSCLE–CALF evokes the body part. Decades of work (Light & Carter-Sobell, 1970; Schvaneveldt et al., 1976; Tulving & Thomson, 1973; and many others) shows that the same word leaves different traces in memory depending on context.

Yet most computational models of memory treat each word as a fixed vector — the same bundle of features every time. Caplan (2023) proposed attentional subsetting: knowledge is high-dimensional, but only a small, context-dependent subset of features is active at any moment. Relational influence (Caplan, Chakravarty, & Dittmann, 2022) is one mechanism that selects which subset: when items A and B appear together, A picks out a distinctive set of B's features that may not have lit up otherwise.

The thesis tests this idea behaviorally. Before getting to the experiments, Chapter 1 builds the model that generates the predictions. That's what we'll explore here.

2. Items as vectors

An item is a column vector of n dimensions. Each dimension is a feature — its value drawn from a Gaussian with mean 0 and variance 1/n. The vector is essentially unique by virtue of its high dimensionality.

3. The bag problem

The classical vector model encodes a list as the sum of its item vectors:

m = f1 + f2 + … + fL

This works for item recognition — to test whether a probe was studied, take the inner product with m; the more the probe overlaps with the bag, the higher the match.

But for pair recognition it fails. Suppose you study two pairs, A–B and C–D:

m = a + b + c + d

Vector addition is commutative. The bag is identical whether the pairs were A–B, C–D or A–D, C–B. The pairing information is gone the moment vectors are summed. So a recombined probe like A–D matches just as well as the genuine pair A–B. Chance performance.

This is why the matrix model was invented. Outer products g f⊤ preserve "g on the left, f on the right" — a sum can't.

The masked vector model takes a different route. Rather than adding explicit binding machinery, it lets the items themselves change shape when paired.

4. The mask

For each studied pair {gi, fi}, define a binary mask wii — a vector of 0s and 1s. A 1 means "this feature is active for this pair"; a 0 means "this feature is silent here." The proportion of 1s is a free parameter, p.

The mask is the relational fingerprint: it captures the subset of fi's features that get activated specifically because gi was sitting next to it.

Top: the raw item f. Middle: the mask w (orange = 1, faded = 0). Bottom: the masked item f ⊗ w — only the features the mask permits survive.

5. Masked encoding

To encode a list of L pairs, sum the masked pair-vectors:

m = Σi=1L (gi + fi) ⊗ wii

Each pair contributes to the bag, but only in the dimensions its own mask selects. The bag now has structure: dimensions that pair 1 wrote into, dimensions that pair 2 wrote into, etc., with overlap depending on the masks.

6. Retrieval: intact vs. recombined

At test, the model is probed with a pair. If the pair's left and right items were studied together, it's intact; if they came from different studied pairs, it's recombined. The matching strength is

s = m · ((g + f) ⊗ w)

For an intact probe, the same mask wii that wrote into the bag is used to read it out. The dimensions match → high inner product.

For a recombined probe, the mask that would govern this never-studied pairing (wji) is different — it picks out different dimensions than what was actually written into the bag for this pair. Most of the dimensions the probe is "looking at" contain near-zero values → low inner product → correct rejection.

8. Behavioral predictions

Two predictions follow from the masked vector model, both tested in Chapters 2–3:

Item recognition: relational influence hurts hits

Suppose you studied MUSCLE–CALF. The mask selected CALF's "leg-muscle" features. At test, CALF appears alone — without MUSCLE to pull out the leg-muscle face, CALF's dominant "baby cow" features come to mind. The probe and the stored trace activate different dimensions; the mismatch lowers matching strength, and you're more likely to miss a studied item.

Associative recognition: relational influence helps correct rejections

You studied MUSCLE–CALF and GOLF–ARCH. The test asks about GOLF–CALF. The mask this never-studied pair would activate (perhaps the foot-arch features of CALF) sits in different dimensions than the leg-muscle features actually stored. Low overlap → confident rejection.

Same mechanism, opposite consequences. Whether intact participants actually use this mechanism is the empirical question the rest of the thesis tackles — and, spoiler from the abstract: the experimental results were largely null.
